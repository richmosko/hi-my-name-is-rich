Spent the past 12 days taking a side trip out to an island in the Caribbean... you know the one: about 50 miles west of Haiti, 50 miles north of Jamaica, and 90 miles south of Key West. Cuba: only a leap of faith / short flight away from Cancun. This was one of the main destinations that we wanted to see but arriving in Mexico, we had absolutely no idea how we would get there. For those of you who have been sleeping under a rock for the last 50 years or so, it's worth pointing out that Cuba is a difficult place to visit as an American. A crippling trade embargo imposed by the US mandates that it is illegal for Americans to spend money in Cuba or on Cuban goods. This in essence makes it illegal to go there, though ironically it's not strictly illegal to be in Cuba... Sabes? There are certain ways around this restriction, mostly involving arriving via a 3rd country like Canada or Mexico.

Anyway, as I was saying earlier, this is a restriction that limits Americans in a few ways:

1. you can't fly directly from the US. This was not that much of a problem for us, as we were in Mexico anyway. The flight from Cancun is a little over an hour long.
2. You cannot get your passport stamped upon arrival. The Cuban immigration officials are well aware of this and customarily stamp your entry visa instead.
3. ATM cards issued by US banks do not work. This is really inconvenient because it means that you need to plan ahead and bring along any/all cash that you might need. You probably want Euros for this, as $US dollars have a steep 10% fee imposed by the Cuban government for currency conversion.
4. Credit cards issued by US banks do not work. This means that you can't purchase airline tickets online, can't reserve or book hotels in advance, and can't rent cars or anything that requires a credit deposit. This is the big one that gets you. It basically means that you have to book your entire trip through a 3rd party travel agent outside of the US. This is actually a good thing because it means no purchases are recorded in US dol.

With all this in mind, we walked around Playa del Carmen shopping for travel agents who could set everything up for us. We settled on a small husband and wife outfit called Solatino... mostly because they did not charge us a 3-5% fee for using our Visa card (we paid them half in cash). This was a little bit complicated, as we had to arrange to pick up paper vouchers for our pre-paid hotels and flights. Seriously: No electronic ticketing... Cuba is a very "special" country after all.

![Havana](/images/albums/RTW-Caribbean/IMG_2213.JPG)

We started off in the capital city Havana for 3 nights of sightseeing and walking around. We saw a bunch of the more famous landmarks out here... more so we could check them off a list than anything else. One of the more memorable highlights was that we saw a live performance of the remaining members of the Buena Vista Social Club, held in a old colonial rum distillery (now converted into a museum). This had a little bit of the late "John Lee Hooker" feel, as these guys are pretty old now: more than half of the band is comprised of young musicians, and the principles looked like they had seen better days. We were pretty surprised to find however, that these guys could still sing and perform at an extremely high level. We have a video uploaded of some of this, so you can check it out for yourselves.

Next up we spent 3 nights further west in Viñales: a small town in a valley overlooking the mountains. I had hoped to visit the vega (tobacco plantation) de Robaina in Pinar del Rio to see the art of growing and fermenting tobacco for cigars, but it turns out that region was over 100 km away and we were visiting in the wrong season. As such, most of the fields were growing corn or yucca because the tobacco growing season is in the winter. So instead, we went to visit some nearby caves and did some horseback riding which was surprisingly fun. We did get to visit a local vega while horseback riding... a bonus that we hadn't planned on. It was quite interesting to hear about the tobacco growing process and how the different leaves get used to fabricate cigars. We even got the requisite hand rolled cigar to taste test. Very nice.

![Trinidad](/images/albums/RTW-Caribbean/IMG_2388.JPG)

Traveled east from here to the beach to visit the well preserved colonial town of Trinidad. Fueled by the riches of sugar barons and built on the blood, sweat, and tears of thousands of imported slaves, Trinidad feels transported from centuries long past. It's kind of amazing that buildings around here never got modernized, as even the cobblestone streets have an old and rustic feel. Walking around, I tried to keep tally and was convinced that horses/bicycles outnumbered cars by a large margin. While here, we went on a hike in the Topes de Collantes... pretty challenging as that Cubans somehow have no idea what a switchback is. Trails pretty much go straight up and down the mountains. Beautiful country, though.

We read the guide books and they say again and again that the people are extremely friendly. Well, it's absolutely true... Everyone has been really nice and we never felt like we had to worry about getting ripped off or anything. Sure, there were some random touts trying to steer us to a particular bar or restaurant... but they were pretty harmless: mostly looking for a free drink from the bar owner for bringing in some money. And make no mistake... we were obviously money walking. Nowhere is this more obvious than heading away from the hotels to grab a bite to eat. Dinner for two, with drinks: $3 USD. I think we might have even been over charged.

Check out the [wiki link on Cuba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuba)... It's an amazing read even if you are familiar with some of the more recent historical events.
